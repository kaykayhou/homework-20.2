Summary

Busy parents can have a hard time keeping track and communicating expectations and chores to their children, creating tension in the home. Hippo aims to develop a solution for busy parents to help their kids complete their household chores in a fun and innovative way. Hippo’s engaging and interactive model creates a platform that encourages family collaboration and positive reinforcement.

User Insight

Busy parents needs to have a easy way to communicate expectations with their kids because of their family's busy work/school schedules.

Problem Statement

Hippo is being designed to find an easy, chore-tracking solution for busy parents. We observed that the majority of family stress stems from conflicting schedules, miscommunication, and responsibilities.

How might we design an app that improves communication between family members, decreases nagging, and keeps their child more accountable?

Feature Prioritization



The ability to set due dates to chores

The ability to assign a family member to a chore

Easily track rewards and progress

Flexible reward options for completed chores

User Journey

We wanted our solution to bring peace and harmony to chaotic circumstances. This kicked off how we started to design the user flow.

User Flow

When designing the user flow, we wanted to make the interface as easy to navigate as possible. After discussion, we decided against a home page, and instead to house all the functions in a button navigation toolbar. After the user is signed in, the landing page becomes the chore page to make marking a completed task as quick as possible.

Wireframes

After iterating the user flow we divided up the wireframes by function, and each group member was tasked with the paper prototype for that function. After all the screens were completed we combined them all into one Figma board and made it a clickable prototype.

Prototyping



User testing + Iterations

We conducted 2 user tests using the lo-fi prototype. Each user was given 5 tasks to complete. Both users completed all the tasks successfully, but we gained some valuable feedback and insights that caused us to reevaluate some of our navigation and flow.

In our user tests, the users were confused by the rewards options, and some had trouble assigning a reward to a chore.

Wireframes

After in depth discussion, our team hashed out what needed to be done to resolve the problem. We reworked some of the screens, and adjusted what functions were connected where.

We added a separate tab just for rewards tracking, which has both individual and family rewards tracking. We also simplified the reward options when users are creating a new chore.

After making the alterations, we conducted mroe user tests, and the new results showed 100% task completion rate, and users easily understood the different reward capabilities.

Style Guide



Mid-fidelity

From our paper-prototype, we decided to go straight to mid-fidelity. We incorporated iOS HIG design patterns into our mid-fi, and started refining our design from there. We did two more user tests with our mid-fi prototype.

In our final user tests, we did not identify any other painpoints. Our final iterations were mainly involving UI finetuning.

Lessons Learned for the Group:

Managing the scope of the project As a team, we learned the discipline of narrowing down our ideas to be able to create a high quality final product in the alotted time.

The importance of user testing at early stages It was through conducting user testing early on in the wireframing process that we were able to identify major user flow problems. This saved us a lot of time and headache.

Going Forward:

We would like to conduct more research from a child’s perspective to enhance the user experience from their point of view.
